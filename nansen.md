I am currently a Research Scientist working in the Data Assimilation group at the
Nansen Environmental and Remote Sensing Center in Bergen, Norway.
Broadly speaking, I am interested in developing data assimilation and machine learning systems that
can constrain weather and climate predictions toward the most realistic
estimate of the coupled Earth system.
My background is interdisciplinary, and I enjoy integrating advancements from
mathematics and computational science to a variety of applications,
from medium-range weather forecasting to reanalysis production.

Before joining the Nansen Center, I worked in the
Modeling and Data Assimilation Division
of NOAA's Physical Sciences Lab in Boulder, Colorado, USA.
At PSL I worked on a variety of projects aimed at designing machine learning weather
emulators for weather forecasting and data assimilation applications.
I worked with a range of prediction systems, from Lorenz-type
idealizations of weather dynamics to NOAA's operational forecasting system.

I obtained my Ph.D. in Computational Science, Engineering, and
Mathematics in 2021 from the
Oden Institute at The University of Texas at Austin.
My graduate work focused on quantifying uncertainties that are inherent to
ocean models, and I implemented a generic, adjoint-based framework to propagate
these uncertainties onto predictions from the
MIT general circulation model.
I used this framework to show the degree to which sparse hydrographic observations
reduce uncertainty in simulation-based estimates of ocean driven melting underneath
the Pine Island ice shelf, which is fed by one of the fastest
flowing glaciers in Antarctica.
The results showed how valuable observations in this region are for constraining modeled quantities.
